Identification of the topic


Our idea was to search for information on Palazzo d'Accursio within ArCo and identify any gaps or areas that could be enriched. Before starting the research, we wrote an ASK query to verify the existence of Palazzo d'Accursio in the ArCo database.


PREFIX arco: <https://w3id.org/arco/ontology/arco/>
PREFIX rdfs: <http://www.w3.org/2000/01/rdf-schema#>
ASK
WHERE {
?culturalProperty rdfs:label "Palazzo d'Accursio".
}


Then, we verified whether a picture of Palazzo d'Accursio was present with an OPTIONAL pattern.

PREFIX rdf: <http://www.w3.org/1999/02/22-rdf-syntax-ns#>
PREFIX rdfs: <http://www.w3.org/2000/01/rdf-schema#>
PREFIX arco: <https://w3id.org/arco/ontology/arco/>
PREFIX a-cd: <https://w3id.org/arco/ontology/context-description/>
PREFIX foaf: <http://xmlns.com/foaf/0.1/>

SELECT *
WHERE {
?cp a arco:ArchitecturalOrLandscapeHeritage ;
rdfs:label ?l .
FILTER(REGEX(?l, "palazzo d'accursio", "i"))
FILTER(REGEX(?l, "bologna", "i"))
OPTIONAL { ?cp foaf:depiction ?depiction }
}


Extraction of IRI and information


After verifying the existence of Palazzo d’Accursio and examining related images, we formulated a query to retrieve its exact IRI.

PREFIX arco: <https://w3id.org/arco/ontology/arco/>
SELECT DISTINCT ?cp
WHERE {
?cp a arco:ArchitecturalOrLandscapeHeritage ;
rdfs:label ?l .
FILTER(REGEX(?l, "Palazzo d'Accursio", "i"))
}
LIMIT 10


This led us to the IRI of Palazzo d’Accursio: https://w3id.org/arco/resource/ArchitecturalOrLandscapeHeritage/0800262039. From there, we began exploring its ontologies and the various pieces of information associated with it.

COMMENTS ON THE 3 DIFFERENT TRIPLES AND BEHAVIOUR OF LLMS

The SPARQL CONSTRUCT query created by ChatGPT states that the subject hall has two predicates: the label “Sala Urbana” and the predicate expressed by the property “isPartOf”. It also includes a BIND clause, which is not necessary. It also does not add the indication that the label is in Italian.
The SPARQL query created by Gemini gives uses a shortened version of the IRI of hall and of the IRI of Palazzo D’Accursio, which requires the prefix declaration “PREFIX arco-res: https://w3id.org/arco/resource/”. It states that Sala Urbana is the subject, the property “arco-core:isPartOf” is the predicate, and Palazzo D’Accursio is the object.
The second triple assigns the label “Sala Urbana”, which is a predicate, to the IRI of hall, which is the subject. The indication of the label in Italian was not present here either. The section WHERE is rightfully empty because the query is not looking for existing data to match, it is included solely to comply with the correct syntax.
The query created by the LLM mistral is longer than the other two. It uses, like Gemini, the shortened version of the IRI of Palazzo D’Accursio and of hall. There is the addition of a variable “?type” to replace with the class to which Sala Urbana belongs to. The WHERE block contains four BIND statements.
These bind statements are used to assign fixed values to variables, this makes the query reusable. Respectively, the bind statements assign the IRI of hall to the variable “?subject”, the IRI of Palazzo D’Accursio to the variable “?object”, the property “isPartOf” to the variable ?predicate”, and the string “Sala Urbana” to the variable “?label”.

Investigation with Large Language Models


To further explore the presence of Sala Rossa, we queried three LLMs (ChatGPT, Gemini, Mistral) using different prompting techniques.


Zero-Shot Prompting


Prompt: Does Palazzo d’Accursio contain a hall called “Sala Rossa”?

Responses:

  • ChatGPT (Ai): Yes, Palazzo d’Accursio in Bologna contains the Sala Rossa (“Red Hall”), historically used for official receptions and decorated with red damask. The hall is notable for its ornate furnishings and the artworks that highlight its political and ceremonial importance.
  • Gemini: Yes. The Sala Rossa is part of Palazzo d’Accursio, located in the civic heart of Bologna. It is a representative hall known for its red décor and for hosting institutional events.
  • Mistral: Yes, the Sala Rossa belongs to Palazzo d’Accursio. It is an important ceremonial space with strong artistic relevance, characterized by its red interiors and historical decorative program.

Chain-of-Thought Prompting

COMMENTS ON THE 3 DIFFERENT TRIPLES AND BEHAVIOUR OF LLMS


The first triple, proposed by ChatGPT , states that the building has a distinct physical object (DecorativeApparatus) identified by a URI. COMMENTS ON THE 3 DIFFERENT TRIPLES AND BEHAVIOUR OF LLMS


When we compared the three proposed queries for modeling the relationship between the "statue of Pope Gregory XIII" and "Palazzo d’Accursio" in the ArCo Knowledge Graph, I noticed that only Chatgpt's triple was valid in SPARQL. The reason lies in how each query understands the difference between "classes, instances, and identifiers" in an ontology.
The first query works because it creates a new instance of the class "statua". In other words, it does not confuse the general concept of a statue with the specific statue that actually exists in Bologna. By assigning it a stable IRI "statua-PapaGregorioXIII", the query makes it possible to uniquely identify this cultural property inside the graph. On top of that, it gives the instance a label: “Papa Gregorio XIII”, which is the human-readable name, and correctly relates it to the Palazzo d’Accursio using the property `arco-core:isPartOf`. This way, both humans and machines can understand that the real statue is part of that architectural heritage site.
Mistral's proposal, by contrast, makes a crucial ontological mistake: it takes the class definition of “statue” and treats it as if it were the actual statue of Pope Gregory XIII. As a result, the model collapses the distinction between a general category and an individual cultural object, which is semantically incorrect.
Gemini’s version, on the other hand, tries to create the statue but does so with a blank node instead of a proper IRI. A blank node is anonymous — it says “there is something here,” but without giving it a permanent name. This makes the data unstable, because the statue cannot be referenced or linked consistently in other parts of the graph. In addition, Gemini’s query uses a property that is not part of the ArCo ontology (`hasCulturalProperty`), which makes it incompatible with the expected data model.
In short, only the first query respects the logic of the ontology: it introduces a new instance with a stable IRI, provides a label for human readability, and uses the correct ontology relation to connect it to Palazzo d’Accursio. The other two either confuse concepts (Mistral) or create unstable, non-standard data (Gemini).

Faced challenges


Several factors made the execution of this project particularly challenging. First of all, the ArCo SPARQL endpoint often proved unstable: on one occasion, it was completely unusable for several hours, and in many cases, even when provided with correct queries, it failed to return results within the given time limit. As a result, we had to wait considerable amounts of time to obtain responses and frequently had to rerun the queries at different times.

A second difficulty lay in identifying suitable elements to propose as additions to Palazzo d’Accursio within ArCo. We realized that the knowledge graph does not include IRIs for many historical figures for artists like Prospero Fontana. This limitation made it impossible for us to suggest triples that incorporated this type of information. Consequently, we chose to focus on more generic elements—such as statues and rooms—for which we were able to find IRIs, and which we then enriched with descriptive labels in the proposed triples.

Large Language Models (LLMs) turned out to be a double-edged sword: on the one hand, they were useful, but on the other, they often proved unstable and unreliable. Some answers concerning precise information were inaccurate, and certain triples they generated contained predicates not present in the ArCo ontology. To address these issues, we manually verified the information using reliable sources and carefully checked the proposed triples against the ArCo ontologies to identify and correct the errors.
